About this project

A distributional approach to measuring grammatical category overlap across Universal Dependencies treebanks.

What this is

Part-of-speech categories are usually treated as discrete: a word is either a NOUN or a VERB, never partly both. In practice, lexical items often sit between categories: gerunds between noun and verb or participles between verb and adjective. This approach quantifies that ambiguity at the level of an entire treebank.

For each treebank, we build a feature vector per (lemma, POS) unit from its UD dependency annotations, compute cosine-similarity neighbourhoods, and aggregate the result into two complementary views: a stability matrix (how homogeneous each category's neighbourhood is) and an intruder matrix (which other category each one most often leaks into).

How it works

1. Feature vectors

Each lexical unit is represented as a vector over the morpho-syntactic features that appear in its syntactic context in the treebank. The syntactic context is defined as the parent, the children, and the node immediately before and after.

All features on these nodes are selected.Units with fewer than ten occurrences are dropped to limit noise. This creates a feature matrix where the lines are lexical units and the columns are extracted features in the format "parent.upos=VERB" or "child.Number=Sing". The values are the proportion of occurences of that lexical unit that have that feature.

2. Neighbourhoods

For every lexical unit we compute cosine similarity to every other unit in the treebank, and keep the top 20 neighbours whose similarity exceeds 0.7. The neighbourhood is the unit's distributional signature.

3. What causes the overlap?

Bridge words are lexical units responsible for the overlap between two categories. The annotation scheme places them in category A, but distributional data pulls them towards category B. To isolate them, we calculate two measures for each lexical unit:

  • Purity: the proportion of neighbours that share the unit's own category.
  • Entropy: the distributional spread of the unit's neighbours across all categories.
The image below shows the intuition behind these two measures. For each bridge word we also report the pulling features: the individual feature dimensions on which the word resembles the rival category's prototype most strongly.

4. Stability and intruder matrices

Aggregating across all units, we build a square category-by-category matrix. The stability matrix shows the internal cohesion of categories. The higher the value on the diagonal, the more cohesive the category is. The intruder matrix is the same matrix with the diagonal removed and rows renormalised, which makes the strongest rival category for each row stand out visually.

5. Weighted Average Stability

A single scalar per treebank: the diagonal of the stability matrix weighted by each category's relative frequency. WAS ranges between 0 and 1; values close to 1 mean every category has a tight, homogeneous neighbourhood, while lower values indicate more cross-categorical overlap. It's a quick comparable summary across treebanks, with the understanding that what it summarises is always one specific distributional view of a treebank's annotation.

Interpreting the results

WAS scores depend on three things: the language, the treebank's annotation style, and its size. The token count is shown next to every treebank for exactly this reason. Smaller treebanks produce noisier feature vectors and less reliable neighbourhoods, so their scores should be read with more caution.

For each language, the largest available treebank was selected. When comparing two treebanks of very different sizes, the WAS difference is partly a real linguistic signal and partly a statistical artefact of the smaller one.

Note: A low WAS doesn't necessarily indicate a problem with the treebank, as some languages and annotation schemes naturally permit more categorical fluidity than others. The bridge word explorer is the right place to investigate what's actually happening.
